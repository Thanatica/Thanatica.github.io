The Tsyanfan Abjad

1. Method

The Tsyanfans's writing system isn't an alphabet. Rather, it is an abjad like Arabic and Hebrew. This means that their writing system does not require the vowels to be written, and often times, only the consonants are written while the vowels are inferred. Unlike most modern abjad writing systems, the Tsyanfans's writing system is a pure abjad, meaning there are no optional vowels or vowel diacritics which aid the reader. There are absolutely no written representations of for their vowels.

For the Tsyanfan people, while it can be challenging when two dialects clash, this system of writing has been reaffirmed over the centuries of linguistic evolution by the practical methods by which they write. Instead of chiseling stone, carving wood, or inking paper, they use nails (called "shung" or ) hammered into a piece of wood and wrap string around them made of different materials depending on which cultural regions of Tsyanf the author originates. The string could be wool, sinew, or rope; it just had to be a fitting width for the dimensions of the 3x3 grid that would contain a single letter. Symbols are created through the patterns created by the string connecting from nail to nail.

To safe space and nails, the letters may be written on top of each other on the same grid, but this is usually saved for really short words or artistic pieces. More often than not, the borders of each 3x3 grid overlap each other to create a unified string of lines. People who grew up with this system can intuit almost instantly the amount of nails and length of string required for the sentences they want to write.
