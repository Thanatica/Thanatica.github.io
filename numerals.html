The Savilsh Number System

1. Base 5

Savils have a positional number system, meaning the value of a symbol is based on the position it has in relation to other symbols. This is how it works in English's Arabic numerals. For instance, "1" can be equal to a single entity, but in "15," it represents ten entities. However, unlike traditional English counting, the Savils naturally count in base 5. They do this by stringing squares in the four corners of the 3x3 grid using the middle point, adding a square with every number. Although over a century of occupation and international trade, there is an ongoing shift towards counting in base 10; so there is a traditional way of counting used in the country-side, and there is a modern way of counting in business sector. This shift impacts the grammar and includes borrowed words that don't align with the usual phonotactics of the Salvish language. Numbers 1-5 are simple to remember with their short and unique names, but the rules begin to get more complicated there after, especially after 10 (or 20 in base 5).

Before explaining the patterns in the Savilsh number system, I need to lay the groundwork by explaining English's. Numbers 1 through 12 in English have unique names (remnant of a base 12 system), and then after, numbers end in " -teen." Think of " -teen" as a short hand for " + 10." Thirteen is "3 + 10," and fifteen is "5 + 10." Once we reach twenty, there is a new suffix, " -ty." Instead of addition, this is multiplication, or " • 10." Twenty is "2 • 10," and forty is "4 • 10." The Savilsh numbers follow a same pattern but in base 5, where "-" is " + 5" and "-" is " • 5."

While numbers 1 through 5 have unique names with their own vowels, when adding a suffix for " + 5," the root word will change its vowel to the suffix. Imagine instead of "fifteen," it is feefteen." When adding the suffix for " • 5," the suffix will change its vowel to the root word's. Imagine instead of "twenty-five," it is "twenty-fehv."
